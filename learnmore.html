The Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) effectively explains the behavior of gases. The Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT) consists of several postulates:

A. Gas is composed of large number of particles, either atoms or molecules. They are so small compared to the distance separating them that their own size can be considered negligible. Compared to the total gas volume, the particles occupy negligible volume. A considerable empty space exists between particles.

B. Gas particles move randomly in straight line motion and collide with each other and the container frequently. The force of collisions of the gas particles with the walls of the container causes pressure.

C. Collisions of gas molecules are perfectly elastic. This means no energy is lost as friction when molecules collide.

D. Gas particles exhibit almost negligible attractions and repulsions for each other. The molecules thus, move freely as far as space will allow.

E. The average kinetic energy of gas molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (Kelvin). At the same temperature, the molecules of all gases will have the same average kinetic energy.

Macroscopic Properties of Gases

Types

Pressure

Pressure is the force exerted per unit area.
Units: Pascals (Pa), Atmosphere (atm), Millimeters of mercury (mmHg), and Pounds per square inch (psi).

KMT Postulate: Postulate B states that gas particles are in constant random motion and collide with each other and the walls of the container.

Relationship to Pressure: These collisions are the source of pressure. The more frequently and forcefully gas particles collide with the container walls, the higher the pressure.

Volume


Volume is the amount of space that a substance occupies.
Units: Liters (L), Milliliters (ml), Cubic meters (m³), and Cubic centimeters (cm³).

KMT Postulate: Postulate A describes gas particles as being very small compared to the distance between them, meaning there's a lot of empty space.

Relationship to Volume: This empty space allows gases to be highly compressible. Reducing the volume of a container forces the gas particles closer together, increasing the frequency of collisions, and thus increasing pressure.

Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance.
Units: Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit(°F), and Kelvin (K).

KMT Postulate: Postulate E establishes that the average kinetic energy of gas molecules is directly proportional to absolute temperature.
Relationship to Temperature: Higher temperature means faster-moving gas molecules. This increased kinetic energy leads to:


- More frequent collisions: The faster molecules collide more often with the container walls, increasing pressure.
- More forceful collisions: The collisions themselves are more forceful, further increasing pressure.

Apply What You Have Learned!

Instructions: Fill your mouth with air. Press your cheeks with your fingers.

1. Did the air occupy space in your mouth?

You were able to fill your mouth with air, indicating that the air took up space within your mouth.

2. Were you able to press your cheeks with your fingers?

You could press your cheeks, indicating that they were not empty but filled with something (air).

3. When you pressed one of your cheeks, did you feel the air moving to the other side of your cheek?

The air, being compressible, moved to the other side of your cheek when you pressed on one side. This further proves that air occupies space and is capable of moving to accommodate pressure.

Did You Know? (Click Me!)
You are constantly surrounded by trillions of tiny air particles that are moving so fast they would make a supersonic jet look like a snail.
Trivia Question 1: Why does a balloon filled with air get smaller when it's cold?
Answer: KMT explains this! Lower temperatures mean gas particles (like the air in the balloon) have less kinetic energy, moving slower and colliding less frequently. This reduced movement leads to a decrease in volume, making the balloon shrink.
Trivia Question 2: Why does perfume smell stronger in a warm room?
Answer: Again, KMT! Higher temperatures mean the perfume's particles (which are gases) have more kinetic energy, moving faster and spreading out more quickly. This increased movement and dispersion make the scent more noticeable.
Trivia Question 3: Why does a bicycle pump get hot when you pump up a tire?
Answer: The act of pumping increases the pressure and kinetic energy of the air particles inside the pump. These collisions between the high-energy particles generate heat, making the pump feel warmer.
